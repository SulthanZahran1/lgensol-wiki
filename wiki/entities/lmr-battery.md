# Lithium Manganese Rich (LMR) Battery

## Overview

Lithium Manganese Rich (LMR) batteries represent a next‑generation cathode chemistry that aims to bridge the gap between low‑cost LFP (lithium iron phosphate) and high‑energy NCM (nickel‑cobalt‑manganese) systems. By substantially increasing manganese content (60–65%) and virtually eliminating cobalt, LMR delivers a unique balance of energy density, safety, and sustainability. The chemistry enables theoretical capacities above 250 mAh/g—approaching NCM levels—while maintaining competitive material costs comparable to LFP. LMR is positioned as a key technology for mainstream electric vehicles (EVs), especially as automakers seek to reduce dependence on expensive and ethically challenging cobalt.

## Chemistry and Composition

LMR cathodes are built on a layered lithium‑rich structure with the general formula xLi₂MnO₃·(1‑x)LiMO₂ (M = Ni, Mn, Co, or combinations). The high manganese content stabilizes the structure via Mn⁴⁺ ions, which provide excellent thermal stability at elevated temperatures. Critically, LMR cathodes are designed to be cobalt‑free or very low cobalt (Co‑free), leveraging abundant global manganese reserves (estimated at 1.5 billion tons) to secure stable, low‑cost supply chains.

Unlike conventional intercalation cathodes (e.g., NCM, LFP), LMR accesses extra capacity through oxygen redox activity at high voltages (~4.5 V vs. Li/Li⁺). This mechanism involves reversible oxygen oxidation (O²⁻ → O⁻) that contributes an additional ~100 mAh/g beyond the transition‑metal redox. The lithium content is also higher—approximately 8 % by weight in the cathode—compared to less than 2 % in LFP, which improves recyclability economics.

## Performance and Safety

- **Energy Density:** LMR cells achieve practical energy densities of 160–210 Wh/kg, roughly 33 % higher than LFP (120–160 Wh/kg) and comparable to mid‑nickel NCM. The high capacity (>250 mAh/g) translates into longer driving range for EVs.
- **Voltage Profile:** The average voltage of LMR is ~3.6–3.8 V, slightly lower than NCM but higher than LFP (~3.2 V). However, a gradual voltage fade over cycling remains a technical challenge.
- **Thermal Stability:** The stable Mn⁴⁺ ions in the layered structure suppress oxygen release even at high temperatures. This makes LMR inherently safer than high‑nickel NCM, which can undergo thermal runaway above 200 °C. LG Energy Solution’s Safety Reinforced Separator (SRS®) technology—a ceramic‑coated separator capable of withstanding up to 200 °C—can further enhance safety when paired with LMR.

## Sustainability and Recycling

LMR offers clear environmental advantages:

- **Cobalt‑free formulation** eliminates the ethical and ecological risks associated with cobalt mining in the Democratic Republic of Congo.
- **High lithium content (≈8 %)** and the presence of nickel and manganese make recycling economically viable. In contrast, LFP contains only ~2 % lithium and low‑value iron, making recycling unattractive.
- **Manganese abundance** ensures long‑term supply stability without geopolitical vulnerabilities.

LG Energy Solution’s LMR designs target a closed‑loop recycling model, where valuable metals (Li, Ni, Mn) are recovered with high efficiency. This aligns with global regulations requiring battery recyclability.

## Technical Challenges and Solutions

Two primary obstacles must be overcome for commercialization:

1. **Low Initial Coulombic Efficiency (ICE):** During the first charge, part of the lithium is irreversibly consumed to form the cathode‑electrolyte interphase (CEI) and structural rearrangements. This reduces usable capacity. Surface treatments, such as lithium‑rich oxide coatings or electrolyte additives, can improve ICE by stabilizing the CEI layer.
2. **Voltage Fade:** Continuous cycling causes a gradual drop in operating voltage due to irreversible oxygen loss and structural transformation from layered to spinel‑like phases. Researchers address this through cation doping (e.g., Al, Zr) and nano‑engineering to maintain a stable layered structure.

Recent advances from LG Energy Solution include a proprietary LMR + silicon anode combination, protected by core patents (awarded the “2026 Invention King Gold Prize”). This pairing leverages the high capacity of silicon (3,600 mAh/g) to offset ICE losses and achieve overall higher system energy.

## LG Energy Solution’s Role

LG Energy Solution has been a pioneer in LMR development. In May 2026, the company recognized Dr. Park Byung‑chun (Cathode Technology Lead) with the Gold Prize for “LMR Cell Chemistry” patents. Notably, LG filed key patents before the industry fully embraced LMR, establishing a strong intellectual property moat. The company is also collaborating with General Motors (GM) to produce prismatic LMR batteries for next‑generation electric trucks and large SUVs.

Beyond the cathode, LG’s SRS® separator and advanced electrolyte additives (e.g., those improving high‑temperature endurance) are integral to the LMR ecosystem. These components, together with a deep understanding of CEI formation (see [[cei|CEI]]), enable cycle life and safety targets required for automotive applications.
