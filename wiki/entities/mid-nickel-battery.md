# High-Voltage Mid-Nickel Battery

## Overview

High-voltage mid-nickel batteries represent a strategic compromise in lithium-ion cathode chemistry, using a moderate nickel content (40–60%) combined with an elevated operating voltage (4.35–4.4 V) to deliver a balanced mix of energy density, cost, and safety. Developed by LG Energy Solution (LGES) as the core chemistry for its **Standard** product segment, this battery type is positioned between premium high‑nickel NCMA (nickel‑cobalt‑manganese‑aluminum) and affordable LFP (lithium iron phosphate). It addresses the mainstream EV market—vehicles in the 300–500 km range class—where battery cost and performance must be carefully optimized to meet automaker requirements and consumer expectations.

The fundamental principle behind the high-voltage mid-nickel approach is the energy density equation: **Energy Density = Voltage × Capacity**. By increasing the operating voltage beyond the conventional 4.2 V of standard NCM (nickel‑cobalt‑manganese) chemistries, the battery compensates for the lower specific capacity (mAh/g) that results from reducing nickel content. This voltage uplift is enabled by advanced cathode structuring and electrolyte formulations that maintain stability at higher potentials, avoiding parasitic side reactions that degrade performance. The result is a cell that approaches the energy density of high‑nickel systems—typically 650–700 Wh/L at the cell level—while offering better thermal stability and a lower bill of materials.

## Technical Details

### Chemistry Composition and Voltage Trade‑offs

Mid‑nickel NCM cathodes are formulated with nickel content in the 40–60% range, with correspondingly higher manganese (30–50%) and reduced cobalt (10–20%). The increased manganese fraction improves structural stability and safety by suppressing oxygen release at elevated temperatures, while the lower cobalt content directly reduces material cost and geopolitical supply chain risk. To counteract the capacity penalty of lower nickel—which would otherwise yield ~160 mAh/g versus ~195 mAh/g for a high‑nickel NCM811—the battery is operated at 4.35–4.4 V. At these voltages, the cathode can extract additional lithium without significant structural degradation, thanks to the stabilizing effect of manganese and the use of single‑crystal particle morphology.

Typical mid-nickel compositions include NCM523 (50% Ni, 20% Co, 30% Mn), NCM613 (60% Ni, 10% Co, 30% Mn), and NCM424 (40% Ni, 20% Co, 40% Mn). LGES’s **Pure NCM** variant uses NCM613 and won the Best Innovation Award at InterBattery 2024, highlighting the industry recognition of high‑voltage mid‑nickel as a distinct and commercially viable cathode class.

### Single‑Crystal Cathode Technology

A critical enabler of high‑voltage operation is the shift from conventional polycrystalline (aggregate) cathode particles to single‑crystal morphology. In a polycrystalline particle, multiple primary crystals are fused together with grain boundaries that act as weak points under stress. At high voltages, repeated lithium intercalation/deintercalation causes anisotropic volume changes that crack these grain boundaries, exposing fresh surfaces to electrolyte and accelerating impedance growth and capacity fade. **Single‑crystal cathodes** avoid this by using discrete, individual crystal particles that have no internal grain boundaries. The resulting microstructure is significantly more resistant to microfracture, offering:

- **~30% improvement in cycle life** (e.g., maintaining 80% capacity after 1,500 cycles versus 1,000 cycles for polycrystalline equivalents).
- **~10% higher energy capacity** because less lithium is trapped in cracked, electrochemically inactive regions.
- Improved safety, as fewer cracks reduce the risk of internal short circuits and thermal runaway.

Synthesis of single‑crystal NCM typically uses a flux‑assisted calcination process that promotes the growth of large, well‑faceted particles (3–5 µm) without agglomeration. This process also allows a narrower particle size distribution, leading to more uniform electrode coating and better rate capability.

### Electrolyte and Cathode‑Electrolyte Interphase (CEI)

Operating at 4.35–4.4 V places the cathode potential near the oxidative stability limit of standard carbonate electrolytes. To prevent excessive electrolyte decomposition, high‑voltage mid‑nickel cells rely on specially formulated electrolytes with additives that form a robust **cathode‑electrolyte interphase (CEI)**. Fluorinated solvents (e.g., FEC, FEMC) and additives like lithium bis(oxalato)borate (LiBOB) or prop‑1‑ene‑1,3‑sultone (PES) are used to create a thin, ion‑conductive passivation layer on the cathode surface. This CEI suppresses transition metal dissolution (particularly manganese and cobalt) that would otherwise contaminate the anode and degrade performance. The effectiveness of the CEI is a key differentiator: well‑designed CEI layers can reduce impedance growth by up to 50% over life compared to conventional electrolyte systems.

### Safety and Thermal Stability

High‑voltage mid‑nickel batteries offer superior safety compared to high‑nickel chemistry due to the higher manganese content (60 mol% Mn in some formulations) and the structural robustness of single‑crystal particles. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) data shows that delithiated mid‑nickel cathodes have an onset temperature for oxygen release that is 20–30 °C higher than NCM811 (ca. 240 °C vs. 210 °C). Furthermore, LGES integrates its **Safety Reinforced Separator (SRS)**—a ceramic‑coated polyethylene separator with enhanced thermal shrinkage resistance—to further mitigate short‑circuit risks. Together, these features enable the cell to pass nail penetration and overcharge tests even at the higher voltage.

### Energy Density and Cost Metrics

At the cell level, LGES targets **670 Wh/L** for its high‑voltage mid‑nickel NCM batteries. This is achieved through a combination of high‑voltage operation, optimized electrode densification, and single‑crystal cathode morphology. The cell gravimetric energy density is typically in the range of 250–270 Wh/kg. Compared to high‑nickel NCMA cells, the mid‑nickel chemistry offers:

- **30% better cell stability** (reduced gas generation and capacity fade).
- **~8% cost reduction** at the pack level, stemming from lower cobalt content and the ability to use cheaper **lithium carbonate** (Li₂CO₃) instead of lithium hydroxide (LiOH) for cathode synthesis. Lithium carbonate is approximately 20–30% less expensive and has a more stable supply chain.

## Market Significance

High‑voltage mid‑nickel batteries fill an important gap in the EV battery portfolio. While [[high-nickel-battery|high‑nickel batteries]] dominate the premium, long‑range segment (600 km+), and [[lfp-battery|LFP batteries]] serve the entry‑level and commercial vehicle sectors (300 km range), mid‑nickel targets the growing **standard EV segment** that demands 400–500 km of range at a competitive price point. This segment is expected to represent the largest volume in the global EV market by 2027, especially in Asia and Europe.

LGES’s strategy positions high‑voltage mid‑nickel alongside its other chemistries in a tiered product lineup: high‑nickel for premium, mid‑nickel for mainstream, and LFP for cost‑sensitive applications. The technology is already being qualified by multiple OEMs for mass‑production vehicles scheduled for 2025–2026. Its energy density and safety profile also make it attractive for **energy storage systems (ESS)** where cycle life and safety are paramount.

Competing technologies such as [[ncm-battery|NCM batteries]] and [[ncma-battery|NCMA batteries]] are also evolving, but mid‑nickel’s combination of moderate nickel content, reduced cobalt, and high voltage offers a unique sweet spot. The development of dual‑layer single‑crystal cathodes and advanced electrolyte systems may push energy density beyond 700 Wh/L in subsequent generations, further narrowing the gap with high‑nickel.
