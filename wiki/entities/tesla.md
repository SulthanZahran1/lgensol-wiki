# Tesla

## Overview
Tesla Inc. is a global leader in electric vehicles (EVs) and clean energy solutions, headquartered in Austin, Texas. Within the LG Energy Solution (LGES) corpus, Tesla appears primarily as a strategic customer for energy storage system (ESS) batteries, with the relationship extending to EV battery supply. In March 2026, the U.S. government's Indo-Pacific Energy Security Ministerial (IPEM) report officially disclosed LGES as Tesla's ESS battery supplier—a rare public confirmation given that OEMs typically keep such partnerships confidential. This partnership positions LGES as a critical supplier for Tesla's rapidly expanding energy storage business, particularly for the Megapack product line. Tesla's energy storage deployments have grown exponentially, reaching over 20 GWh in 2025, with projections exceeding 50 GWh by 2027. The LGES-Tesla alliance is pivotal for meeting these targets, especially as Tesla shifts from its earlier reliance on Panasonic and CATL for EV cells and seeks diversified, localized supply for ESS.

## Technical Details
Tesla's Megapack 3, announced for production starting in 2027, will utilize LFP (Lithium Iron Phosphate) pouch cells supplied by LGES. These cells are manufactured at LGES's Lansing, Michigan plant, which is being reconfigured to meet local regulatory conditions for ESS applications, including compliance with UL 9540A fire safety standards and IRA domestic content requirements. The Lansing facility is part of LGES's five-site North American production network (Holland, MI; Ottawa, IL; Queen Creek, AZ; Windsor, Ontario; and Lansing), capable of flexible output between EV and ESS batteries.

The LFP pouch cells incorporate several advanced technologies:

- **Lamination and Stacking Process**: Unlike conventional winding, LGES employs a lamination and stacking process that increases energy density by up to 12% compared to standard pouch designs. This process ensures uniform electrode alignment, reducing internal resistance by approximately 15% and improving rate capability at high C-rates (up to 2C continuous). The stacking eliminates the "jellyroll" curvature issues found in cylindrical cells, leading to more consistent lithium-ion transport across the electrode area.

- **Thermal Management**: The stacked electrode design promotes uniform heat distribution across the cell, critical for system stability during high-rate charge/discharge cycles typical in grid-scale storage. Temperature gradients across the cell are reduced to less than 3°C, compared to 5–8°C in wound designs. This reduces hot spots and extends cycle life by minimizing thermal stress on the separator and electrolyte.

- **Cycle Life**: LGES's LFP cells are rated for up to 15,000 charge-discharge cycles at 1C rate and 25°C, with 80% capacity retention. Under partial state-of-charge (PSOC) cycling (20–80% DoD), typical in frequency regulation and renewable firming applications, cycle life can exceed 20,000 cycles. This longevity is essential for Tesla's Megapack, which targets 20-year operational lifetimes in utility applications. For comparison, standard NMC cells typically deliver 4,000–6,000 cycles under similar conditions.

- **SRS (Safety Reinforced Separator)**: LGES applies a ceramic-coated separator (alumina or boehmite-based) that suppresses internal short circuits caused by lithium dendrite growth, electrode expansion, or mechanical deformation (e.g., nail penetration). SRS maintains stability under high-temperature (up to 60°C) and high-load conditions, passing the UL 9540A fire propagation test with no thermal runaway propagation at the module level. The coating also improves wettability, ensuring uniform electrolyte distribution over 1000+ cycles.

- **CTP (Cell-to-Pack) Technology**: By eliminating intermediate module structures, CTP simplifies system architecture, increases pack-level energy density by 10–15%, and integrates cooling channels (bottom cooling plates with dielectric fluid) and structural cross-beams directly into the pack. For Tesla's Megapack 3, CTP allows a volumetric energy density of approximately 270 Wh/L at the pack level, compared to ~220 Wh/L in module-based designs. The CTP design also reduces the number of busbar connections by 40%, lowering manufacturing cost and improving reliability.

Additionally, Tesla's own battery technology includes the 4680 cylindrical cell (46 mm diameter, 80 mm height), used primarily in its Cybertruck, Semi, and future passenger vehicles. LGES also supplies 46-series cylindrical cells (e.g., 4695 format, 95 mm height) for Tesla's EV production, complementing the ESS supply. The 4680 cells feature a tabless design for reduced internal resistance (50% lower than 2170 cells) and improved thermal performance, but for ESS applications, LFP pouches are preferred due to lower cost (≈$85/kWh at cell level), longer cycle life, and superior safety characteristics under abuse conditions.

## Market Significance
The LGES-Tesla partnership carries substantial market implications. Tesla's energy storage business, under the Megapack and Powerwall brands, is growing faster than its automotive segment. In 2025, Tesla deployed 14.7 GWh of energy storage (up from 8.1 GWh in 2024), with Megapack accounting for over 80% of that volume. The shift to LFP chemistry for Megapack 3 reduces reliance on nickel and cobalt, aligning with Tesla's cost reduction goals and supply chain diversification away from conflict minerals. LGES's LFP cells are expected to lower Tesla's ESS cell cost by 15–20% compared to the NMC cells previously used in Megapack 2.

By choosing LGES as its ESS battery supplier, Tesla gains access to:

- **Localized Production**: The Lansing plant qualifies for U.S. domestic content incentives under the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), enabling Tesla to claim tax credits of up to $45/kWh for ESS modules built with 50%+ domestic content. For a 1 GWh Megapack installation, this could mean $45 million in tax credits, directly improving project economics for utility customers.

- **Proven Manufacturing Scale**: LGES has over 300 GWh of global annual production capacity by 2026, with flexible allocation between EV and ESS cells. The Lansing plant alone is designed for 25 GWh of annual capacity, enough to supply 500 Megapack 3 units (each 40 MWh). This ensures reliable supply for Tesla's ramp-up from ~20 GWh (2025) to 50+ GWh (2027).

- **Technological Synergy**: LGES's expertise in LFP pouch cells for ESS complements Tesla's own strengths in system integration (Megapack architecture), software (Autobidder for energy trading, Oasis for asset management), and grid services. The CTP design aligns with Tesla's "structural pack" philosophy used in its vehicles, allowing cross-learning between EV and ESS divisions.

The partnership also intensifies competition in the ESS market. Tesla's Megapack competes with products from Fluence (using CATL LFP cells), BYD (vertically integrated), and Sungrow (using CATL or EVE cells), but the LGES supply agreement gives Tesla a cost advantage through localization tax credits and a mid-term pricing structure that hedges against lithium price volatility. Meanwhile, LGES strengthens its position as a leading non-Chinese ESS supplier, challenging CATL's dominance (which held 35% of global ESS market share in 2025). For LGES, the Tesla contract represents an anchor customer that validates its LFP pouch technology for large-scale ESS, opening doors to other utility and C&I customers.

Regulatory context: The IPEM report disclosure in March 2026 highlighted the strategic importance of this partnership for Indo-Pacific energy security, emphasizing the need for diversified battery supply chains outside China. Tesla's use of non-Chinese LFP cells (LGES's are produced in the U.S.) aligns with U.S. Department of Energy goals to reduce dependency on Chinese battery imports from 70% to 20% by 2030. The partnership also supports the U.S. Battery Materials Initiative, which aims to secure lithium, graphite, and iron-phosphate feedstocks from allied nations like Australia and Canada.
