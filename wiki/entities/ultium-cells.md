# Ultium Cells

## Overview
Ultium Cells is a 50/50 joint venture between LG Energy Solution (LGES) and General Motors (GM), established in 2019 to manufacture advanced battery cells for GM’s Ultium electric vehicle (EV) platform. The venture is the cornerstone of GM’s electrification strategy, providing a dedicated, high-volume supply of pouch cells tailored to the automaker’s modular battery architecture. Ultium Cells operates two large-scale manufacturing plants in the United States—in Lordstown, Ohio and Spring Hill, Tennessee—with a third plant under construction in Lansing, Michigan. The facilities represent a combined investment exceeding $7 billion and are designed to produce over 70 GWh of battery cells annually by mid-decade, making Ultium Cells one of the largest battery manufacturing operations in North America. The joint venture also received a $2.5 billion loan from the U.S. Department of Energy’s Advanced Technology Vehicles Manufacturing (ATVM) program, underscoring its strategic importance for domestic battery supply chain resilience and compliance with Inflation Reduction Act requirements.

## Technical Details
Ultium Cells produces large-format pouch cells using a quaternary NCMA (nickel, cobalt, manganese, aluminum) cathode chemistry. The formulation targets a nickel content of approximately 90%, with cobalt reduced to roughly 5% and aluminum added to enhance structural stability and suppress oxygen release during thermal runaway. This chemistry delivers a cell-level energy density of 260–300 Wh/kg, enabling GM’s Ultium packs to achieve ranges of over 400 miles on a single charge while using fewer cells per pack than earlier designs. The pouch form factor—typically measuring around 600 mm in length and 100 mm in width—allows for flexible stacking within modular battery trays, which can be arranged in vertical or horizontal configurations across GM’s diverse vehicle platforms, including the GMC Hummer EV, Cadillac Lyriq, Chevrolet Silverado EV, and Chevrolet Blazer EV.

Manufacturing at Ultium Cells relies on LGES’s Advanced Z-Stacking (AZS) process, which places electrode layers with micrometer precision to maximize active material loading and minimize internal resistance. Each plant operates “smart factory” quality control systems that integrate real-time monitoring of electrode coating thickness, electrolyte fill volume, and formation cycle parameters. Machine vision and AI-based defect detection identify micro-shorts, pinholes, or delamination before cells leave the production line, achieving rejection rates below 1%. The facilities are also designed for flexible production: lines can switch between EV and ESS (energy storage system) cell chemistries on a weekly basis, allowing Ultium Cells to respond to shifting market demand. For example, if GM’s EV orders slow temporarily, the plants can redirect capacity to produce cells for LGES’s grid-scale ESS customers, such as Tesla or independent utility operators in the United States. This dual-mode capability is a direct response to the cyclical nature of the automotive market and the growing demand for stationary storage, as highlighted by LGES’s expansion of ESS production sites in North America.

The Lordstown plant (Plant 1) began production in late 2022 with an initial capacity of 30 GWh, later uprated to 35 GWh through process optimization. The Spring Hill plant (Plant 2) achieved full-scale production in mid-2024, ramping to 40 GWh in the first year with a target of 50 GWh after final expansions. The Lansing plant (Plant 3) broke ground in 2023 and is expected to add another 50 GWh of capacity by 2026, bringing the venture’s total to over 130 GWh—enough to supply cells for approximately 1.5 million EVs per year. Each plant covers roughly 2.8 million square feet and houses over 500 electrode coating, stacking, and forming modules, many of which are sourced from LGES’s domestic partners to comply with local content requirements.

## Market Significance
Ultium Cells is a bellwether for the battery industry’s shift toward regionalized production and joint venture (JV) structures. Before Ultium Cells, automakers like GM relied primarily on third-party cell suppliers such as LG Energy Solution, Samsung SDI, or SK On with limited strategic control over cell design and supply security. The JV model—where GM provides the pack architecture, cell shape, and thermal management requirements while LGES supplies the cathode chemistry, process technology, and manufacturing know-how—has become a template for subsequent partnerships, including LGES’s collaborations with Stellantis (NextStar Energy) and Honda (in Ohio). By co-locating cell plants near GM’s assembly hubs (Lordstown for the new EV trucks in Detroit and Spring Hill for the Cadillac Lyriq and Silverado EV), Ultium Cells drastically reduces logistics costs and guarantees just-in-time delivery—a critical advantage in an industry where battery shipping accounts for up to 5% of cell cost.

From a policy perspective, Ultium Cells is a flagship project under the Biden administration’s goal to bring critical mineral processing and battery manufacturing back to North America. The $2.5 billion ATVM loan, along with state-level incentives (e.g., Ohio’s Job Creation Tax Credit), enables the venture to compete with lower-cost Asian producers while satisfying the Inflation Reduction Act’s domestic assembly requirement for EV tax credits. As of 2025, cells from Lordstown and Spring Hill meet the IRA’s “made in America” threshold, helping GM qualify for the full $7,500 federal purchase credit on models like the Chevrolet Equinox EV and Cadillac Lyriq. Furthermore, Ultium Cells’ ability to pivot to ESS cells provides a hedge against EV demand volatility—an increasingly important strategy as renewable energy deployment drives North American ESS deployment from 10 GWh in 2023 to an estimated 50 GWh annually by 2027.

The venture also advances NCMA pouch cell technology by demonstrating its scalability. While cylindrical cells (e.g., 4680 format) dominate the Tesla ecosystem and prismatic cells are popular in Asia, Ultium Cells’ pouch cells offer superior space utilization within a flat battery pack (critical for SUVs and trucks) and allow higher volumetric energy density than comparable prismatic designs. LGES’s decision to deploy its AZS stack in the U.S. also demonstrates the feasibility of transferring advanced Korean manufacturing processes to a Western workforce, addressing the perennial challenge of technical talent shortages in battery production.
