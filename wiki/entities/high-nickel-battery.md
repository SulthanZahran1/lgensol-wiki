# High-Nickel Battery

## Overview

High-nickel batteries utilize cathodes with a nickel content of 60% or more (typically 80–90% in modern formulations) in layered oxide structures such as NCM (nickel‑cobalt‑manganese), NCA (nickel‑cobalt‑aluminum), and NCMA (nickel‑cobalt‑manganese‑aluminum). Nickel is the primary redox-active element in these cathodes, undergoing oxidation from Ni²⁺ to Ni⁴⁺ during charge and enabling high reversible capacity. Increasing the nickel proportion directly boosts the energy density of the cell, making high-nickel chemistries the preferred choice for long-range electric vehicles (EVs). However, the pursuit of ever‑higher nickel content brings significant challenges in structural stability, thermal safety, and cycle life, which must be addressed through advanced material engineering.

## Technical Details

### Electrochemical Role of Nickel

Nickel’s ability to cycle between Ni²⁺ and Ni⁴⁺ states allows more lithium ions to be extracted per unit mass of cathode, resulting in higher specific capacity. For example, raising nickel content from 60% (mid‑nickel) to 90% in a NCMA cathode can increase reversible capacity by over 20%. This capacity gain directly translates into longer driving range for EVs—a single charge can exceed 500 km with premium high‑nickel cells.

### Degradation Mechanisms

Despite its capacity advantage, high nickel content introduces intrinsic instability:

- **H2–H3 Phase Transition**: During delithiation at high voltage, the layered structure undergoes a sharp anisotropic contraction and expansion as it transitions from the H2 to the H3 phase. Repeated cycling causes microcracks within secondary particles, accelerating capacity fade.
- **Cation Mixing**: Nickel and lithium ions have similar ionic radii (~0.69 Å vs. ~0.76 Å for Li⁺). In nickel-rich compositions, Ni²⁺ ions can occupy lithium sites, blocking Li⁺ diffusion pathways and impairing rate capability.
- **Surface Degradation**: Highly reactive Ni⁴⁺ at the particle surface reacts with electrolyte, forming a poorly conductive rock‑salt phase (NiO‑like) and causing oxygen loss. This layer increases impedance and contributes to thermal instability.

### Engineering Solutions

To overcome these challenges, manufacturers apply several strategies:

- **Bulk Doping**: Substituting a small fraction of nickel with dopants such as aluminum, zirconium, or titanium strengthens metal‑oxygen bonds and stabilizes the lattice. Doping mitigates the H2–H3 phase transition and reduces cation mixing.
- **Surface Coating**: Applying a protective layer (e.g., alumina, lithium niobate, or a conductive polymer) suppresses electrolyte side reactions, minimizes oxygen release, and reduces rock‑salt phase formation. Uniform coating processes ensure consistent protection.
- **Single‑Crystal Morphology**: Conventional polycrystalline cathodes are composed of many small crystallites that are prone to intergranular cracking. Single‑crystal cathodes—where each particle is a single grain—exhibit greater resistance to fracture and better structural integrity. LG Energy Solution is advancing from small‑particle single‑crystals (few micrometers) to medium‑particle sizes to increase electrode packing density and cell energy density.
- **Precursor Engineering**: Controlling the size distribution and morphology of the precursor (Ni₀.₈Co₀.₁Mn₀.₁(OH)₂, etc.) minimizes stress concentration during cycling, further reducing microcrack formation.

## Significance and LG Energy Solution’s Context

LG Energy Solution (LGES) positions high‑nickel batteries in its Premium segment, targeting maximum range, power, and fast‑charging performance. The company’s flagship chemistry is **NCMA** (nickel ≥89%, cobalt ≤5%, with aluminum added to improve stability and reduce cost). LGES has invested in nickel supply chain security, for example acquiring a 4.8% stake in Greatpower Nickel & Cobalt Materials (China) and signing long‑term offtake agreements for ~20,000 tons of nickel—enough for about 370,000 high‑performance EVs.

To further enhance high‑nickel NCMA, LGES is refining single‑crystal technology. Moving from polycrystalline to single‑crystal particles reduces intergranular cracking and allows higher operating voltages. Multi‑doping (Al, Zr, Ti) and conformal surface coatings are applied in parallel to balance energy density, cycle life, and safety. These innovations enable LGES to reach cell energy densities above 800 Wh/L while maintaining thermal stability comparable to lower‑nickel systems.

High‑nickel batteries remain the dominant choice for premium EVs, but their complexity demands continued investment in material science and manufacturing process control. As LGES scales up its next‑generation NCMA production, the trade‑off between capacity and stability is being progressively narrowed through the strategies described above.
