# Rivian

## Overview

Rivian is an American electric vehicle manufacturer founded in 2009, known for producing the R1T pickup truck and R1S SUV—both designed for adventure and off-road capability. The company went public in 2021 and has since established itself as a prominent player in the premium EV segment. Rivian’s vehicle platforms, particularly the second-generation R1T and R1S introduced in 2024, incorporate next-generation battery technology to improve range, charging speed, and durability. In the LG Energy Solution (LGES) battery ecosystem, Rivian appears as a key customer for the 4695 cylindrical cell format—part of the 46-series family that LGES is ramping for high-volume production. This partnership positions Rivian as an early adopter of LGES’s large-format cylindrical cells, which are designed to replace smaller cylindrical formats like 18650 and 21700 while offering superior energy density and simplified pack architecture. The collaboration carries strategic weight for both companies: Rivian secures a domestic supply chain compliant with the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), and LGES diversifies its customer base beyond Tesla and other pouch-cell clients.

## Technical Details

The LGES 4695 cell supplied to Rivian measures 46 mm in diameter and 95 mm in height, making it one of the tallest variants in the 46-series cylindrical family. (For comparison, the 4680 cell used by Tesla is 80 mm tall; the 4695 adds 15 mm to increase active material volume.) Each cell operates at a nominal voltage of approximately 3.66 V and is designed with a capacity target of 35–40 Ah, yielding a per-cell energy content of roughly 130–150 Wh. At the pack level, a Rivian R1T equipped with the Large pack (135 kWh) would require approximately 900–1,000 cells—dramatically fewer than the 7,776 2170 cells used in earlier Rivian packs. This reduction in cell count simplifies thermal management, reduces the number of welds and electrical connections, and lowers pack-level resistance.

Chemically, the 4695 cells use LGES’s NCMA (nickel-cobalt-manganese-aluminum) cathode formulation. The aluminum doping stabilizes the cathode structure at high states of charge, enabling a nickel content above 85% while suppressing oxygen release and thermal runaway. This chemistry delivers a volumetric energy density of 800–850 Wh/L at the cell level, rivaling the best prismatic cells in LGES’s portfolio. The anode employs a blend of synthetic graphite with up to 3% silicon monoxide (SiO) to boost specific capacity to around 350 mAh/g. A key innovation in the 4695 is LGES’s proprietary tabless electrode design, which reduces internal resistance by eliminating current collectors at the jellyroll ends. The tabless configuration lowers the cell’s impedance to approximately 2–3 mΩ, enabling sustained 2C–3C charging rates (roughly 200–300 kW at the pack level for a 135 kWh battery) without excessive heat generation. Thermal management is further aided by the cylindrical form factor, which provides a continuous surface area for cooling along the side wall—a feature exploited by Rivian’s active liquid-cooled thermal system.

The cells are produced using LGES’s dry coating process for the anode and a solvent-free cathode coating method that reduces manufacturing energy consumption by up to 30% compared to traditional wet slurry techniques. This process improvement is critical for meeting the cost targets demanded by Rivian’s goal of achieving sub-$70/kWh pack cost. Safety validation includes nail penetration, overcharge, and thermal shock tests; LGES claims the 4695 passes these with a <5% failure margin, partially due to the inclusion of a pressure-release vent that activates at 1.5 MPa and a ceramic‑coated separator that resists dendrite penetration.

## Market Significance

Rivian’s adoption of LGES’s 4695 cells validates the 46-series format as a mainstream EV battery solution beyond Tesla’s 4680. While Tesla has pursued an exclusive, in-house approach for its 4680 cells, LGES’s open supply model—supplying customers like Rivian, GM, and Hyundai—creates a competitive ecosystem. The 4695’s taller geometry offers a 15%–20% increase in cell energy relative to a 4680 of the same diameter, making it particularly attractive for vehicles with high energy demands, such as Rivian’s heavy adventure trucks. This differentiation allows Rivian to claim range advantages without adopting prismatic or pouch formats that require more complex structural integration.

From a geopolitical perspective, the LGES-Rivian partnership directly supports compliance with the IRA’s “foreign entity of concern” (FEOC) restrictions and the requirement for 50% of battery component value to be manufactured or assembled in North America by 2024, rising to 100% by 2028. LGES’s Arizona factory, located in Queen Creek, operates as a standalone facility with non-Chinese equipment supply chains; it will produce the 4695 cells exclusively for North American customers, ensuring that Rivian qualifies for the full $7,500 federal EV tax credit. The plant’s planned capacity of 36 GWh per year (enough to supply approximately 270,000 Rivian R1T packs at 135 kWh each) signals deep strategic commitment.

The deal also impacts LGES’s market position. Historically strong in pouch cells (e.g., for the Volkswagen MEB platform), LGES uses the Rivian contract to prove its cylindrical cell manufacturing at scale—a crucial step as the global cylindrical cell market is forecast to grow from 45 GWh in 2024 to 280 GWh by 2030 (BloombergNEF). Rivian’s insistence on dual sourcing (the company also procures 4695 cells from Samsung SDI) forces LGES to compete on cost, energy density, and cycle life. Initial reports indicate LGES’s cells offer 1,500–2,000 cycles to 70% capacity retention under standard 1C discharge, matching or exceeding the Samsung SDI equivalent. This competitive dynamic benefits the entire industry by accelerating improvements in 46-series performance and reliability.
