# NCMA (Quaternary) Battery

## Overview

NCMA (Nickel Cobalt Manganese Aluminum) batteries, also called quaternary batteries, represent the latest advancement in layered oxide cathode chemistry. By introducing aluminum (Al) as a fourth element into the conventional NCM (Ni–Co–Mn) system, NCMA achieves an optimal balance of high energy density, extended cycle life, improved safety, and reduced cobalt content. LG Energy Solution (LGES) has commercialized NCMA as a premium cathode technology, deploying it in cells for global automakers such as General Motors (Ultium platform) and other high-performance EVs.

The defining characteristic of NCMA is its ability to push nickel content above 80% (commonly 80–90%) while maintaining structural integrity at high voltages and temperatures. The addition of aluminum—typically 1–3% of transition metal content—suppresses detrimental phase transitions and oxygen release, overcoming the primary safety and lifetime limitations of earlier high-nickel NCM chemistries. As a result, NCMA cells deliver gravimetric energy densities exceeding 270 Wh/kg (pack-level 200–240 Wh/kg), support fast charging, and achieve cycle lives exceeding 1,000 cycles.

NCMA is classified as a **high-nickel battery** (nickel fraction >60%), placing it in the premium segment of LGES’s battery portfolio alongside [[high-nickel-battery|other high-nickel systems]]. It bridges the gap between the older ternary NCM/NCA and future ultra-high-nickel chemistries.

## Technical Details

### Composition and Structural Role

A representative NCMA cathode composition is Ni₈₀Co₅Mn₅Al₁₀ (often denoted NCMA80), or, in nickel-richer variants, Ni₉₀Co₅Mn₃Al₂. The mole fractions are precisely tuned: nickel provides high specific capacity (~200 mAh/g at 4.3 V), cobalt improves rate capability and electronic conductivity, manganese stabilizes the layered structure during cycling, and aluminum acts as a structural and thermal stabilizer. Compared to first-generation NCM (e.g., NCM333 with 20–30% Co), cobalt content is reduced to 5–10%, lowering material costs by approximately 30% and addressing ethical and supply-chain risks associated with cobalt mining.

Aluminum ions (Al³⁺) incorporate into the transition metal layer, substituting primarily for nickel and cobalt. Because Al–O bonds are stronger than Ni–O or Co–O bonds, the substitution increases the activation energy for oxygen release—a critical failure mechanism in nickel-rich cathodes. In high-nickel NCM, the layered structure undergoes a disruptive H2 → H3 phase transition near 4.5 V, causing anisotropic lattice strain and microcracking. Aluminum doping suppresses this transition, reducing unit-cell volume shrinkage from ~7% (NCM811) to ~4% (NCMA80) and mitigating particle cracking. This is a direct application of **bulk doping** technology, as described in LGES’s high-nickel development strategy.

### Electrochemical Performance

NCMA cathodes achieve a specific capacity of 195–205 mAh/g over a voltage window of 2.5–4.4 V (vs. Li/Li⁺). When paired with advanced graphite or silicon‑anode blends, full cells reach energy densities of 250–300 Wh/kg. The discharge voltage plateau remains flat (~3.7 V), enabling consistent power delivery. Rate capability is excellent: NCMA retains >85% capacity at 1C and >70% at 3C, thanks to low cobalt content that maintains good lithium-ion diffusivity.

Cycle life is significantly improved over high-nickel NCM. In pouch cells using NCMA80 and a graphite anode, capacity retention after 1,000 cycles at 1C/1C (30°C) is typically 85–90%, compared to 75–80% for NCM811 under identical conditions. Aluminum’s suppression of intergranular cracking also reduces electrolyte decomposition on fresh surfaces, lowering self-discharge and gas generation. Thermal stability is enhanced: the onset temperature for exothermic oxygen evolution (measured by DSC) increases from ~200°C for NCM811 to ~240°C for NCMA80, and the peak heat release rate is reduced by 30–40%.

### Single-Crystal NCMA and Advanced Manufacturing

LGES has pioneered **single-crystal NCMA** technology, where cathode particles are formed as monolithic single crystals (0.5–2 μm) rather than polycrystalline agglomerates. Single-crystal NCMA exhibits even lower surface area, reduced electrolyte side reactions, and negligible particle fracturing, resulting in >95% capacity retention after 1,500 cycles. This is achieved through advanced precursor synthesis that controls particle size and morphology, minimizing stress concentration during cycling.

Additional enhancements include **multi-doping** (e.g., with Ti, Zr, or W) and conformal **surface coatings** (Al₂O₃, AlF₃) that further suppress oxygen release and improve high-voltage stability. These techniques are part of LGES’s comprehensive approach to stabilize high-nickel cathodes, as detailed in the source material on high-nickel batteries.

NCMA cathodes are synthesized via co-precipitation of mixed hydroxide precursors (NiₓCoyMnzAl₁₋ₓ₋y₋z(OH)₂) followed by high-temperature calcination (750–850°C) with a lithium source. The process is adaptable to all cell form factors—cylindrical (2170, 4680), prismatic, and pouch—making NCMA highly versatile.

## Market Significance and LG Energy Solution Context

NCMA occupies the premium tier of the cathode hierarchy, positioned above mid-nickel NCM (e.g., NCM523) and far above [[lfp-battery|LFP (Lithium Iron Phosphate)]] in energy density while approaching the safety and cost characteristics that once favored LFP. Its primary market is long-range EVs (500+ km range) and high-performance vehicles where driving range, fast charging, and durability are paramount. LGES supplies NCMA cells through its joint venture Ultium Cells to General Motors for vehicles such as the Chevrolet Silverado EV, GMC Hummer EV, and Cadillac Lyriq.

Compared to [[nca-battery|NCA (Nickel Cobalt Aluminum)]]—the older ternary system without manganese—NCMA offers superior thermal stability and cycle life because manganese provides additional structural support. Manganese also reduces cost sensitivity to cobalt and nickel price fluctuations. Against LFP, NCMA delivers 30–40% higher energy density, enabling smaller, lighter batteries for the same range. The trade-offs are slightly lower absolute safety tolerance under extreme abuse (though significantly better than NCM811) and higher material costs.

LGES has segmented its EV battery portfolio into Premium, Standard, and Affordable lines. NCMA is the cornerstone of the Premium line, optimized for maximum driving range, power output, and fast-charging capability. Future iterations will incorporate nickel fractions up to 94% with ultra-low cobalt (<2%) and advanced coatings, targeting 300+ Wh/kg cells with 2,000-cycle lifetimes. These developments align with ESG goals by reducing reliance on conflict-affected cobalt sources.

The quaternary concept itself is a natural evolution from ternary systems. As noted in LGES’s blog, the addition of a fourth element (Al) to NCM creates a cathode that combines the advantages of both NCM and NCA—high capacity, excellent power, and robust safety—while lowering cost. NCMA’s ability to be used in any form factor further strengthens its market appeal.
