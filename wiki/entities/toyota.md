# Toyota

## Overview

Toyota Motor Corporation, the world's largest automaker by vehicle sales volume, is a strategic customer of [[lg-energy-solution|LG Energy Solution]] (LGES) for lithium-ion battery supply and recycling in North America. This partnership marks a significant shift for Toyota, which historically prioritized hybrid electric vehicles (HEVs) and hydrogen fuel cell technology over battery electric vehicles (BEVs). Through a multi-year supply agreement and a dedicated battery recycling joint venture, Toyota is leveraging LGES’s proven manufacturing scale and advanced cell chemistry to accelerate its BEV rollout in the critical North American market. The collaboration is designed to satisfy local content requirements under the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), secure a resilient supply chain for critical minerals, and meet Toyota’s global target of securing 280 GWh of battery supply by 2030.

## Technical Details

### Battery Supply Specifications

Under the supply agreement, LGES provides Toyota with high‑energy‑density lithium‑ion cells tailored for its first dedicated BEV platform, the e‑TNGA (Toyota New Global Architecture). The primary chemistry supplied is NCMA (nickel‑cobalt‑manganese‑aluminum), which offers a balance of energy density, thermal stability, and cost. The cells employ LGES’s proprietary NCMA cathode composition—typically Ni ≥ 80%, Co ≤ 5%, Mn ~5%, Al ~1%—enabling pack‑level energy densities above 250 Wh/kg. These cells are produced in LGES's North American manufacturing facilities, including its dedicated production lines at the Michigan plant (Holland), which have been allocated to fulfill the Toyota supply agreement. The total committed capacity for Toyota is estimated at 20 GWh per year initially, with options to increase to 40 GWh by 2028.

All cells are manufactured in a pouch format, a form factor that LGES has mastered over millions of production cycles for customers including [[gm|General Motors]] and [[honda|Honda]]. The pouch design offers superior thermal management and packaging flexibility, which is critical for Toyota’s goal of integrating batteries directly into the vehicle floor structure on the e‑TNGA platform. The first model to use these cells is the bZ4X, a compact crossover BEV with a 71.4 kWh net capacity pack (LGES cells) providing an EPA‑range of approximately 252 miles. Future models—such as the bZ3 sedan and a larger three‑row SUV—are expected to use the same cell family, with pack capacities ranging from 60 to 100 kWh.

### Recycling Joint Venture

In addition to the supply agreement, LGES and Toyota established a North American battery recycling joint venture (JV) in 2025. The JV operates a dedicated facility in the US Midwest, co‑located with one of LGES’s manufacturing sites to minimize logistics costs. The recycling process employs a combination of mechanical shredding and hydrometallurgical recovery, capable of extracting >95% of lithium, >98% of nickel, cobalt, and manganese from end‑of‑life battery packs. The recovered materials are re‑introduced directly into LGES’s cathode precursor production, creating a true closed‑loop system. The plant’s initial throughput is 20,000 metric tons of spent batteries per year, scaling to 50,000 metric tons by 2030. This JV reduces Toyota’s exposure to volatile raw material markets and supports its commitment to carbon neutrality across the full vehicle lifecycle.

## Market Significance

Toyota’s partnership with LGES is strategically significant for several reasons. First, it enables the automaker to qualify its BEVs for the full $7,500 IRA consumer tax credit, which requires that a substantial portion of battery components and critical minerals be sourced from North America or free‑trade‑agreement countries. LGES’s existing US manufacturing footprint and its vertical integration into precursor and recycling activities provide a clear path to compliance. Second, the collaboration strengthens Toyota’s position in the competitive North American EV market against rivals like [[ford|Ford]] and [[rivian|Rivian]], who have also secured domestic battery supply agreements. Third, it diversifies Toyota’s battery portfolio beyond its traditional reliance on Panasonic and PEVE (a Toyota‑Panasonic joint venture), reducing single‑source risk.

The recycling JV also positions Toyota as a leader in circular economy practices within the automotive sector. By integrating recycling from the outset, Toyota expects to reduce its cumulative battery material demand by 25% by 2035. Moreover, the JV complements LGES’s expanding global recycling network, which includes facilities in Europe and South Korea, and aligns with Toyota’s broader “Beyond Zero” environmental strategy.
