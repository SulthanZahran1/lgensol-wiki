# Stellantis

## Overview

Stellantis N.V. is a multinational automotive manufacturer created in January 2021 through the merger of Fiat Chrysler Automobiles (FCA) and Groupe PSA. The company owns a portfolio of 14 vehicle brands including Jeep, Ram, Dodge, Chrysler, Peugeot, Opel, Citroën, Fiat, Alfa Romeo, Maserati, and others. As part of its aggressive electrification strategy, Stellantis has committed to achieving 50% EV sales in the United States by 2030 and a global target of 5 million battery electric vehicles (BEVs) per year by the same date. To secure a reliable supply of lithium-ion battery cells for this massive ramp-up, Stellantis has formed strategic joint ventures with leading battery manufacturers, most notably LG Energy Solution (LGES) through the NextStar Energy partnership.

The NextStar Energy joint venture was announced in 2022 and established a state-of-the-art battery manufacturing plant in Windsor, Ontario, Canada. This facility is dedicated to producing pouch-type lithium-ion cells for Stellantis’s North American EV production. The Canadian federal government and the Province of Ontario provided substantial financial incentives—reportedly up to C$15 billion in production subsidies and capital grants—to anchor the project, recognizing its critical role in building a domestic EV supply chain under the Canada-United States-Mexico Agreement (CUSMA). The Windsor plant began operations in early 2025 and achieved a remarkable milestone: producing over 1 million cells within its first three months, demonstrating rapid yield stabilization and manufacturing excellence.

## Technical Details

The NextStar Energy plant manufactures pouch-type lithium-ion cells using LGES’s advanced chemistry, likely based on a high-nickel NCMA (Nickel-Cobalt-Manganese-Aluminum) cathode formulation. This chemistry offers a favorable balance of energy density, power capability, and cycle life, enabling Stellantis EVs to achieve competitive range and performance. Typical pouch cells produced at the facility are expected to have capacities in the range of 60–100 Ah, with an energy density exceeding 700 Wh/L at the cell level. The cells are designed to support fast-charging (up to 350 kW) and deliver over 1,000 deep discharge cycles under normal usage conditions.

The manufacturing process at the Windsor plant follows LGES’s standard high-precision production flow: electrode coating (slurry mixing, slot-die coating, and drying), electrode calendaring, slitting, vacuum drying, cell assembly (stacking of anode, separator, and cathode sheets), electrolyte filling, formation (first charge/discharge cycle to activate the cell), aging, and final testing. The plant employs fully automated production lines with tight process control to minimize defects and ensure consistent cell quality. A key design feature is the ability to switch production between EV and ESS (energy storage system) batteries. This flexibility is enabled by adjustable electrode coating thickness and cell form factor parameters, allowing LGES to balance supply with fluctuating demand across automotive and grid storage markets.

The Windsor facility has an annual nameplate capacity of 45 GWh, enough to power approximately 500,000–600,000 long-range EVs per year. The plant's location in Ontario provides access to low-carbon hydroelectric power, which significantly reduces the carbon footprint of battery production. Additionally, the site benefits from proximity to Stellantis assembly plants in the Midwest, such as those in Detroit, Michigan, and Brampton, Ontario, enabling just-in-time delivery logistics. The factory also incorporates waste reduction systems, including solvent recovery and water recycling, aligning with Stellantis’s Dare Forward 2030 sustainability goals.

## Market Significance

The Stellantis-LGES partnership is a cornerstone of North American battery production capacity. Alongside LGES’s other joint ventures—Ultium Cells with General Motors and its partnership with Honda—the NextStar Energy plant helps LGES maintain a dominant market share in the region. These three ventures collectively represent over 150 GWh of annual capacity across five sites in Ohio, Tennessee, Michigan, and Ontario. This geographic diversification provides resilience against regional disruptions, such as natural disasters or supply chain bottlenecks, and positions LGES to serve the entire North American EV market.

For Stellantis, the Windsor plant is not merely a supply source but a strategic asset. By producing batteries in Canada under CUSMA rules, Stellantis ensures that its EVs qualify for U.S. federal tax credits under the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), which require final assembly in North America and a growing percentage of battery components sourced from the continent. The Canadian government’s incentives effectively lower Stellantis’s battery costs, improving the competitiveness of its EV lineup against Tesla and legacy automakers like Ford and GM.

The rapid production ramp-up at the Windsor plant—exceeding 1 million cells within three months—is a testament to LGES’s manufacturing expertise. It underscores the ability to transfer mature process technology from South Korea to new greenfield sites quickly, a critical advantage for meeting Stellantis’s ambitious EV launch timelines. The flexibility to allocate production between EV and ESS batteries also allows the joint venture to adapt to market cycles, mitigating the risk of overcapacity in a volatile industry.

Looking ahead, the Stellantis-LGES partnership may expand to additional facilities. Analysts have speculated about a second NextStar Energy plant in North America, potentially in Indiana or Mexico, to cover Stellantis’s heavy-duty truck and commercial vehicle segments. Such expansion would further solidify LGES’s leadership and Stellantis’s vertical integration. The success of the Windsor plant will likely serve as a template for future JV operations, with implications for battery technology evolution, supply chain localization, and policy frameworks.
