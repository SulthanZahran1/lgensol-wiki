# Assembly Process

## Overview

The assembly process is the second major manufacturing stage for lithium-ion batteries, converting coated electrode rolls and separator material into a functional cell structure. This stage directly determines the internal architecture, energy density, and mechanical integrity of the finished cell. The method varies significantly by form factor: cylindrical and prismatic cells typically use winding to produce a cylindrical jelly roll, while pouch cells rely on stacking techniques that maximize volumetric utilization. At LG Energy Solution, the assembly process centers on pouch cells and leverages two proprietary stacking methods—**Lamination & Stacking (L&S)** and the newer **Advanced Z‑Stacking (AZS)** —to achieve industry-leading energy density, safety, and throughput.

## Technical Details

### Lamination & Stacking (L&S)

LG Energy Solution’s core stacking technology, L&S, builds up the cell from fundamental units called **mono-cells**. A mono-cell consists of one central electrode (cathode or anode) sandwiched between two separator sheets, forming a *separator–electrode–separator* structure. After the mono-cell is created by lamination (application of heat and pressure to bond the layers), it is repeatedly stacked. The stack is completed with a **half-cell** —two separator sheets and an anode only—to maintain proper polarity. The resulting multi‑layer assembly is called a **stack-cell**.

Lamination ensures precise alignment of electrodes and separators, minimizing internal voids and preventing electrode misalignment. The stacked architecture fills the pouch case volume far more efficiently than a wound jelly roll: because there is no central mandrel or gap, the active material packing density increases by 10–20%. This allows LG to produce pouch cells as thin as 2 mm (ultra‑slim) for consumer electronics up to elongated formats for automotive batteries. The process also reduces internal resistance and improves rate capability by maintaining consistent electrode-to-electrode distances.

### Advanced Z‑Stacking (AZS)

Building on L&S, LG developed **Advanced Z‑Stacking (AZS)** to further enhance safety and manufacturing efficiency. AZS combines the space‑utilization benefits of Z‑Stacking (where electrodes are placed between a continuous Z‑folded separator) with the bonding strength of lamination.

The key innovation is a **Heat Press** step. After the Z‑folded separator layers are alternately loaded with cathode and anode sheets, heat and pressure (typically 80–120°C and several MPa) are applied to fuse the electrode‑separator interfaces. This achieves three critical outcomes:

1. **Elimination of electrode overhang** – Overhang occurs when one electrode protrudes beyond adjacent layers during handling or stacking, creating local pressure points and short‑circuit risks. Heat‑Press bonding locks the layers in place, fundamentally preventing overhang.
2. **Improved dimensional stability** – The bonded stack resists layer shift during transport between process stations, allowing larger batches (multiple stack‑cells) to be moved and processed simultaneously.
3. **Higher throughput** – Because each stack‑cell is consolidated, handling steps are reduced; production lines can operate with shorter cycle times.

AZS is currently in volume production at PT. HLI Green Power in Indonesia, demonstrating its commercial maturity. The technology is also being deployed at LG’s Ochang Energy Plant for pilot‑line validation.

### Winding for Cylindrical Cells

For cylindrical formats (e.g., 4680, 4695), LG uses high‑speed winding. Continuous strips of cathode, separator, and anode are rolled together into a tight jelly roll at speeds of tens of meters per minute. The jelly roll is inserted into a metal can, tab‑welded, and sealed. Winding is inherently faster than stacking but leaves a central void space (the mandrel hole), reducing volumetric energy density compared to stacked designs. LG’s cylindrical cells therefore target applications where mechanical robustness and fast production are prioritized over absolute energy density.

### Post‑Stack Processing

After stack‑cell formation (whether by L&S or AZS), the assembly process continues with **lead welding** —attaching current collector tabs to the electrode terminals. The stack‑cell is then inserted into a pre‑formed pouch pocket (made by compression of multi‑layer aluminum laminate film). The pouch is sealed along three sides using heat‑sealing tools, leaving one side open for electrolyte filling. Electrolyte is injected into the gas pocket (a temporary chamber) and then allowed to wet the electrode pores. Finally, the pouch is vacuum‑sealed, and the cell proceeds to activation (formation and aging). During formation, generated gases accumulate in the gas pocket, which is later trimmed away in a degassing step.

## Significance and LG Context

LG Energy Solution’s assembly innovations directly address the key quality drivers for pouch cells: alignment precision, short‑circuit prevention, and electrolyte wetting uniformity. The L&S and AZS methods have been refined over two decades of production since LG began pouch cell manufacturing in 2000. By combining the high‑energy‑density advantages of stacking with the robustness of lamination, LG maintains a competitive edge in the global pouch market.

The real‑world impact is measurable: AZS reduces internal defect rates by minimizing overhang and improving layer alignment, leading to longer cycle life and better thermal stability. The process also supports flexible production—the same assembly line can switch between ultra‑thin cells for smart devices and large‑format cells for electric vehicles by adjusting stack count and electrode dimensions.

From a manufacturing logistics perspective, AgZ’s ability to transport bonded stack‑cells in larger batches reduces work‑in‑progress inventory and lowers operational complexity. This is particularly valuable in high‑volume Giga‑factories where any increase in throughput directly improves cost per kilowatt‑hour.
