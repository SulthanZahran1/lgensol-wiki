# Battery Critical Mineral Supply Chain

## Overview

Critical minerals — lithium, nickel, cobalt, manganese, graphite, copper, and aluminum — form the physical backbone of all lithium-ion batteries. The supply chain for these materials directly dictates battery cost, energy density, cycle life, safety, geopolitical risk, and environmental footprint. As global battery production scales toward multiple terawatt-hours annually, securing reliable, responsible, and cost-competitive mineral supply has become a strategic priority for battery manufacturers, automakers, and governments worldwide.

## Technical Details

### Lithium: The Charge Carrier

Lithium is the fundamental charge carrier. Two main compounds are used in cathode production: **lithium hydroxide (LiOH)** and **lithium carbonate (Li₂CO₃)**.

- **Lithium hydroxide** is essential for high‑nickel cathodes (NCM, NCA) because it reacts readily with nickel precursors to form the layered oxide structure needed for high energy density. With nickel contents exceeding 60 % in today’s high‑nickel batteries, demand for LiOH has surged.
- **Lithium carbonate** is used for lower‑energy‑density chemistries such as LFP (lithium iron phosphate) and LMO, and for consumer electronics.

Lithium is sourced from brines (salar deposits) and hard‑rock spodumene. Production from brine typically yields carbonate first, which may then be converted to hydroxide. Australia, Chile, and China dominate current production, with Argentina and Brazil adding new capacity. Global reserves are estimated at 89 million tonnes (USGS 2021), but extraction and processing are time‑intensive, making supply inelastic to short‑term demand spikes.

### Nickel: Energy Density Enabler

Nickel is the primary driver of specific capacity in layered oxide cathodes. In NCM and NCA, increasing the nickel fraction (e.g., from 60 % to 80 % or 90 %) raises the deliverable capacity from ~200 mAh/g to over 230 mAh/g. However, higher nickel content introduces **microcracking** due to anisotropic lattice strain during cycling. These microcracks allow electrolyte penetration, accelerating side reactions and capacity fade. Mitigating strategies include concentration‑gradient architectures, where the nickel content decreases toward the particle surface (e.g., a high‑nickel core enveloped by a low‑nickel, manganese‑rich shell), and advanced doping/coating techniques.

Indonesia has become a major processing hub for nickel, using high‑pressure acid leaching (HPAL) to convert laterite ores into battery‑grade nickel sulfate. Russia and the Philippines remain significant ore producers.

### Cobalt: Stabilizer with Supply Risk

Cobalt stabilizes the layered structure, improving cycle life and safety. However, 60 % of global cobalt reserves are concentrated in the Democratic Republic of Congo, and much of the refined material passes through China, which holds nearly 50 % of the global processing capacity. Artisanal mining practices and geopolitical concerns drive the industry toward **cobalt‑free or low‑cobalt** designs. Eliminating cobalt in high‑nickel cathodes reduces structural stability; therefore, strategies such as doping with aluminum (NCA) or manganese (NCMA) are employed to maintain performance while cutting cobalt content below 5 %.

### Graphite and Anode Minerals

Graphite, both natural flake and synthetic, remains the dominant anode active material, with a theoretical capacity of 372 mAh/g. Natural graphite production is concentrated in China (over 70 % of global output), prompting efforts to diversify sources and to develop synthetic graphite alternatives. **Silicon** (theoretical capacity ~3,600 mAh/g) is increasingly blended with graphite to boost anode energy density, while **lithium metal** (3,860 mAh/g) is pursued for next‑generation batteries, though it faces dendrite‑related challenges.

### Supply Chain Dynamics

Mineral supply chains are plagued by long lead times, geographic concentration, and price volatility. For example, lithium carbonate prices rose more than fivefold between 2021 and 2022 before correcting. Cathode active material represents about 52 % of total battery cell cost, and within that, lithium alone accounts for 60–70 % of the cathode material cost. Such cost sensitivity drives the search for alternative chemistries (LFP, LMFP, sodium‑ion, lithium‑sulfur) and robust recycling ecosystems.

## Significance and LG Context

LG Energy Solution’s mineral strategy reflects a multi‑pronged approach:

- **Chemistry diversification**: The company produces high‑nickel NCM, NCMA (quaternary, adding aluminum to NCM), and increasingly LFP cells. This hedges against price spikes in any single mineral.
- **Long‑term sourcing**: LG Energy Solution has secured multi‑year supply agreements with Compass Minerals (lithium carbonate, 40 % of annual output for seven years starting 2025) and Vulcan Energy (lithium hydroxide, 45,000 tonnes over five years starting 2025). Both suppliers emphasize environmentally friendly extraction (geothermal brine and direct lithium extraction).
- **Regional processing**: Building cathode and battery plants in key markets (e.g., the US, Europe, and Asia) reduces logistics exposure and qualifies for local content incentives.
- **Recycling**: Partnerships with companies like Li‑Cycle enable recovery of lithium, nickel, cobalt, and copper from end‑of‑life batteries, creating a secondary supply stream. LG Energy Solution targets 100 % closed‑loop recycling for its production scrap.
- **ESG commitments**: The company prioritizes suppliers that minimize carbon footprint (e.g., Vulcan’s zero‑carbon lithium project) and adhere to responsible mining practices in cobalt supply chains.

These actions are essential because the global battery industry is projected to require over 1,300 GWh of cell capacity by 2030 (enough for 36 million EVs), and known cobalt reserves alone can only supply about 11 years of current demand projections. Hence, reducing cobalt and diversifying mineral dependencies is not merely an economic choice but a strategic necessity.
