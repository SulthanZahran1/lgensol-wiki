# Separator

## Overview/Introduction

The separator is a porous membrane positioned between the cathode and anode in a lithium‑ion battery. Its dual role is to physically prevent direct electrode contact while allowing lithium ions to pass through its electrolyte‑filled pores. A direct short between the electrodes generates intense heat, which can trigger thermal runaway. The separator is therefore the battery’s most critical safety component. LG Energy Solution has invested heavily in separator innovation, notably the Safety Reinforced Separator (SRS®) coating technology and, more recently, the upcycling of waste separator materials into everyday goods under the RE:ALL campaign.

## Technical Details

### Three Essential Properties

A high‑quality separator must satisfy three interdependent requirements.

**1. Thermal stability and electrical insulation.** The separator must withstand elevated temperatures without shrinking or melting. Most polyolefin‑based separators (PE, PP) have a shutdown function: at around 130–150 °C, their pores close, halting ion transport and cutting off the current. However, the separator must maintain its mechanical integrity after shutdown to prevent electrode contact. Electrical insulation is also critical – the separator must not react with lithium ions or other species inside the cell.

**2. Uniform and numerous pores.** Pores act as channels for lithium‑ion conduction. Their size distribution directly affects current density uniformity. Non‑uniform pores create local current hotspots, accelerating aging and capacity fade. A high porosity (typically 40–60%) and tortuosity factor determine the ionic resistance. The wet process yields more uniform pores than the dry process because it stretches the film biaxially.

**3. Thin yet strong.** Thinner separators allow more active material in the same volume, raising energy density. Modern separators are as thin as 7–12 µm. Yet they must resist puncture by electrode particles, lithium dendrites, and handling stresses during cell assembly. Tensile strength and puncture resistance are key mechanical metrics.

### Dry vs. Wet Manufacturing

Separators are made from polyethylene (PE) or polypropylene (PP) using two main processes.

**Dry process:** A polymer film is extruded and then stretched in one direction (uniaxial) to create elongated pores. The process is simpler and cheaper, but pores are less uniform, and the film thickness is difficult to reduce below 20 µm. Dry separators are used in applications where energy density is less critical, such as ESS (Energy Storage Systems) and electric buses.

**Wet process:** PE or PP is mixed with paraffin oil at high temperature, extruded into a sheet, and then cooled. The oil is extracted, leaving pores. The film is stretched biaxially (both machine and transverse directions), producing a more uniform pore structure and enabling thicknesses down to 5–7 µm. The wet process yields higher energy density and is preferred for EVs, smartphones, and laptops. According to SNE Research, the wet separator market is growing faster than dry, driven by the demand for high‑energy cells.

### SRS (Safety Reinforced Separator)

LG Energy Solution developed SRS® in 2004 to address the intrinsic thermal weakness of polyolefin separators. The technology coats ceramic particles (e.g., alumina or boehmite) and a polymer binder onto one or both sides of the separator. This coating provides two major benefits:

- **Exceptional thermal stability.** Conventional separators begin to degrade above 100 °C; SRS‑coated separators can withstand temperatures up to 200 °C. The ceramic layer acts as a heat‑resistant barrier, preventing the separator from shrinking and causing internal shorts.
- **Enhanced mechanical strength.** The ceramic coating reinforces the separator against puncture by electrode particles or lithium dendrites. This is especially critical for high‑nickel NCM and NCA cathodes, which operate at higher voltages and generate more heat.

SRS is now a key enabler of LG’s high‑energy‑density cells, allowing the company to push nickel content beyond 80% without compromising safety. The technology is in mass production and applied across many premium EV battery lines.

### Separator in Solid‑State Batteries

In solid‑state batteries, the solid electrolyte can partially or fully replace the polymer separator. Some designs use a thin layer of solid electrolyte as both the ion‑conducting medium and the physical separator, eliminating the need for a separate polymer membrane. This simplifies cell architecture but introduces challenges around interfacial resistance and mechanical integrity. LG Energy Solution is actively developing solid‑state cells that incorporate SRS‑like ceramic coatings to improve interface stability.

## Significance / LG Context

LG Energy Solution has leveraged separator innovation as a competitive differentiator. The SRS coating, now in mass production, has been adopted across many of its high‑energy‑density cell lines, including those for premium EVs. The company also recognizes the environmental footprint of separator waste. In 2024, it launched the “RE:ALL” campaign, upcycling waste separator material (polyolefin‑based) into crossbody bags, tablet pouches, and book covers. These goods are made in partnership with recycling firm Right Route, using a patented process that converts separator scraps into “Texnic” fabric – lightweight, durable, and waterproof. The campaign, named “BetteReturn” (meaning “Return Better”), sells the products to LG employees, and proceeds fund ecosystem restoration projects. This initiative reflects LG’s commitment to a “Perfect Closed Loop” for battery materials, extending the separator’s life cycle from safety component to sustainable consumer goods.
