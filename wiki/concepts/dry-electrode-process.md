# Dry Electrode Process

## Overview

The **dry electrode process** is a manufacturing method for lithium-ion battery electrodes that eliminates all liquid solvents from the mixing and coating steps. Instead of the conventional wet process—which mixes active materials, conductive additives, and binders into a slurry using toxic solvents like NMP—the dry process combines these solid components directly into a powder. This powder is then formed into a free-standing film or directly applied to the current collector via roll pressing, bypassing the energy-intensive drying ovens that dominate conventional production lines.

The dry process addresses three critical pain points of wet electrode manufacturing: enormous energy consumption (drying ovens can exceed 100 meters in length and account for ~40% of total battery manufacturing cost), environmental and safety hazards from solvent handling, and structural limitations in thick electrode designs. By removing solvent, the dry process enables higher active material loading, reduces capital expenditure, and cuts carbon emissions—making it a key enabler for next-generation batteries and ESG goals.

## Technical Details

### How the Dry Process Works

The dry electrode process begins with dry mixing of active material (cathode or anode powders), conductive carbon, and binder—typically polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE). Unlike wet processing where the binder is dissolved in solvent, dry mixing relies on mechanical shear to **fibrillate** the PTFE particles. Under high shear, PTFE deforms into microfibers that entangle with active material and conductive additive particles, forming a continuous, three-dimensional network. This network acts as a physical scaffold that holds the electrode together and maintains electrical pathways even at high loading.

After fibrillation, the dry powder is either calendered into a free-standing film (the "Maxwell approach" used by Tesla) or directly coated onto the current collector foil (powder spray coating). In both variants, the final step is roll pressing to achieve the desired density and thickness. No solvent, no drying oven, and no solvent recovery system are required.

### Advantages Over Wet Processing

1. **Elimination of Binder Migration**: In wet coating, as solvent evaporates in the oven, the dissolved binder tends to migrate toward the surface (binder migration), creating a concentration gradient. This weakens adhesion near the current collector and can cause delamination, especially in thick electrodes. The dry process avoids migration entirely because there is no liquid phase to carry the binder; the fibrillated PTFE remains uniformly distributed throughout the electrode thickness.

2. **Higher Energy Density**: Dry electrodes can be made thicker (higher active material loading per unit area) without the structural defects that plague thick wet electrodes. More active material per cell translates directly to higher energy density. The uniform distribution of binder and conductive additive also improves ionic and electronic conductivity, further boosting performance.

3. **Dramatic Cost Reduction**: Eliminating the drying oven and solvent recovery equipment reduces capital investment and floor space. LG Energy Solution estimates total electrode manufacturing cost can be reduced by 17% to 30%. The process also speeds up production—drying time, which accounts for over 90% of electrode manufacturing time, is completely eliminated.

4. **Environmental Benefits**: The most common solvent, NMP, is classified as a reproductive toxin and requires careful handling, recovery, and disposal. Removing NMP eliminates VOC emissions and reduces energy consumption for solvent distillation and incineration. This directly supports corporate ESG targets, such as LG Energy Solution's commitment to 2050 carbon neutrality and 2030 RE100.

## Significance & LG Context

LG Energy Solution has been developing dry electrode technology for approximately 10 years. The company operates a pilot line at its Ochang Energy Plant, was expected to complete pilot-line construction by Q4 2024, and targets commercialization by 2028, with subsequent rollout to global production sites. A key differentiator claimed by LG is that its dry process is applicable to both cathode and anode regardless of particle size—a challenge for some competing approaches. The company is building a patent portfolio around fibrillation optimization and tensile strength improvement.

The dry electrode process is a central pillar of LG Energy Solution's broader ESG and manufacturing strategy. The company's "We CHARGE toward a better future" ESG vision includes 7 core tasks, among them reducing greenhouse gas emissions through process innovation. Replacing wet electrode lines with dry lines directly reduces energy intensity and eliminates solvent waste, aligning with the 2050 carbon neutrality target. Additionally, the reduced manufacturing cost helps make batteries more affordable, accelerating the transition to electric vehicles.

Compared to other process innovations (e.g., [[dld|DLD (Double Layer Slot Die Coating)]] for high-speed coating or [[pre-dispersion]] for improved slurry homogeneity), dry electrode is considered a more disruptive step change because it redefines the entire mixing and coating paradigm. It is particularly relevant for [[46-series-battery|46-series cylindrical batteries]] (4680/4695) where thick electrodes are essential to achieve energy density targets.
