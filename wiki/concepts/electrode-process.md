# Electrode Process

## Overview

The electrode process is the first major stage of battery manufacturing, transforming raw powders and metal foils into coated cathode and anode rolls ready for cell assembly. It consists of five sequential sub-processes: mixing, coating, drying, calendering (roll pressing), and slitting/notching. The quality of the electrode process determines capacity uniformity, resistance, adhesion, porosity, and subsequent electrolyte wetting behavior—all of which directly affect final cell performance.

## Technical Details

### Mixing
Mixing creates the slurry by combining active material, conductive additive, binder, and solvent in precise ratios. The process begins with pre-dispersion of conductive additives to break up agglomerates and ensure uniform distribution, followed by binder dissolution and active material addition. Typical solid content for cathode slurries is around 50–70% by weight, depending on the active material type. The mixing speed, temperature, and duration must be carefully controlled—excessive temperature (above 50°C) can cause binder gelation, while excessive shear can fracture active material particles. For NMP-based systems, solvent recovery systems capture over 99% of evaporated solvent for reuse, but the energy cost remains significant.

### Coating
The slurry is coated onto current collector foil (aluminum for cathode, copper for anode) using slot-die coating. Coating thickness uniformity is critical: a variation of just ±1 μm can translate into a ±2% capacity variation in the finished cell. LG Energy Solution’s Double Layer Slot Die (DLD) coating, first applied to anode slurries in 2018, enables simultaneous application of two different slurry compositions in a single pass. This allows a functional gradient: a bottom layer optimized for adhesion and electrical contact with the current collector, and a top layer designed for fast ion transport and reduced interfacial resistance. DLD reduces binder usage by up to 20% while improving adhesion strength by 30%, enabling 20-minute fast charging capability.

### Drying
The coated foil passes through long drying ovens, often exceeding 100 meters in length, operating at 100–150°C. Drying accounts for over 90% of total electrode manufacturing time and approximately 40% of total battery manufacturing cost. The drying profile must be carefully controlled to prevent binder migration—where dissolved binder moves toward the surface with evaporating solvent, creating a binder-rich skin and a binder-depleted region near the current collector. This phenomenon reduces adhesion and increases interfacial resistance. Multi-zone drying ovens with staged temperature profiles help minimize migration, but the fundamental limitation remains for thick electrodes.

### Calendering (Roll Pressing)
Calendering compresses the dried electrode coating between heavy steel rollers to achieve target density (typically 2.2–2.6 g/cm³ for NMC cathodes and 1.4–1.6 g/cm³ for graphite anodes). The applied linear pressure can exceed 1000 kN/m. This step improves adhesion between the coating and current collector, reduces electrical resistance by compressing conductive networks, and creates the correct pore structure (25–35% porosity) for electrolyte wetting. Over-calendering can crush active material particles and reduce porosity below 20%, impairing wetting and rate capability.

### Slitting and Notching
Slitting cuts the wide electrode roll into strips matching the cell width, typically using rotary knives or laser cutting. Notching creates the tab patterns and V-grooves needed for electrical connections. Laser notching has become preferred over mechanical cutting because it produces cleaner edges with less debris and burr formation—critical for preventing internal short circuits. Notching also defines the uncoated “tab” region where current collectors are welded to the cell terminals. Modern laser notching systems achieve cutting speeds above 20 m/min with edge quality deviations under 10 μm.

## Significance/LG Context

LG Energy Solution has played a pioneering role in electrode process innovation. The company developed and commercialized DLD technology in 2018—the first in the industry—and has since applied it across most of its electric vehicle battery production. DLD enables fast charging performance and improves electrode mechanical integrity.

Looking forward, LG Energy Solution is actively developing the dry electrode process, which eliminates solvent and drying steps entirely. In the dry process, active material, conductive additive, and binder (typically PTFE) are mixed as dry powders and formed into a freestanding film or directly coated onto current collectors. PTFE undergoes fibrillation during mechanical shearing, creating a continuous binder network that enhances structural integrity while allowing reduced binder content. The dry process is expected to reduce electrode manufacturing costs by 17–30%, eliminate NMP solvent handling, and enable thick electrode designs (>200 μm) without binder migration issues. LG Energy Solution plans to build a pilot line at its Ochang Energy Plant by Q4 2024 and commence commercialization by 2028.
