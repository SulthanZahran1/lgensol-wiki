# Conductive Additive (CNT)

## Overview

Conductive additives are essential components in lithium‑ion battery electrodes. They create the electronic pathways that enable efficient charge transport between the [[active-material]] and the [[current-collector]]. Many active materials—such as NCM cathodes or silicon anodes—have inherently low electrical conductivity; without a percolating conductive network, electrons become trapped, leading to high internal resistance and poor power performance. Among the various conductive additives, carbon nanotubes (CNTs) have emerged as a breakthrough material, offering superior conductivity at significantly lower loadings compared to conventional carbon black. CNTs are one‑dimensional nanostructures formed by rolling graphene layers into seamless cylinders with diameters of about 1 nm—roughly 1/100,000th the thickness of a human hair. Their tensile strength is approximately 100 times that of steel, and their electrical conductivity approaches that of copper.

## Technical Details

### Structure and Properties

CNTs are classified by the number of concentric graphene layers. **Multi‑walled CNTs (MWCNT)** consist of several graphene shells nested together, making them thicker, stiffer, and excellent for heat conduction—ideal for cathode applications where mechanical robustness and cost‑effectiveness are priorities. **Single‑walled CNTs (SWCNT)** are a single graphene layer, offering superior flexibility and conductivity. Their high aspect ratio and ability to form entangled, resilient networks make them especially suited for [[silicon-anode]] formulations. During charge–discharge cycling, silicon particles undergo extreme volume expansion (up to 300%); SWCNT networks accommodate this expansion without fracturing, maintaining electrical contact and preventing capacity fade.

### Comparison with Carbon Black

Conventional carbon black particles are spherical and form point‑contact networks. In contrast, CNTs create line‑contact networks that lower the percolation threshold significantly. At equal conductivity, CNT usage can be reduced to about 20 % of the carbon black loading. This reduction frees up volume for additional active material, directly boosting energy density. Furthermore, CNT networks exhibit roughly 10 % higher conductivity than carbon black at equivalent loadings.

### Dispersion and Processing

CNTs tend to agglomerate into bundles due to strong van der Waals forces, making uniform dispersion essential. In the [[pre-dispersion]] step of the mixing process, CNTs are pre‑mixed with solvent and dispersant, then mechanically separated using bead mills or jet mills. Inadequate dispersion leaves agglomerates that impede electron transport and increase resistance, degrading battery performance. LG Energy Solution emphasizes rigorous pre‑dispersion to ensure homogeneous slurry quality, which directly impacts coating uniformity and final electrode performance.

### Role in Silicon Anodes

Graphite anodes possess reasonable intrinsic conductivity and require minimal conductive additive. However, the industry push toward higher energy density has accelerated development of silicon‑based anodes. Silicon’s extreme volume change during lithiation/delithiation causes particle fracture and loss of electrical contact. Anode formulations relying on silicon therefore depend heavily on CNT conductive additives—especially SWCNTs—to maintain a robust conductive network that withstands mechanical stress. This synergy is critical for enabling the high capacity of silicon without sacrificing cycle life.

## Significance and LG Context

### Market Outlook

According to SNE Research, the global conductive additive market for batteries was approximately 650 billion KRW in 2021 and is projected to reach 1.78 trillion KRW by 2025 and 3.07 trillion KRW by 2035, representing an 11.7 % CAGR. Notably, SWCNT demand is expected to surge roughly 300‑fold between 2025 and 2035, while MWCNT demand will triple—reflecting the growing adoption of silicon anodes and high‑power applications.

### Dry Electrode Process Integration

LG Energy Solution is actively developing CNT‑based conductive additives and dry electrode processes to reduce manufacturing costs and enhance battery performance. In the dry electrode process, active material, conductive additive, and binder are mixed as a dry powder without solvent, eliminating the drying step and reducing energy consumption by up to 40 %. CNT additives play a critical role here because they form efficient conductive networks even in thick, dry‑coated electrodes, avoiding the binder migration issues seen in wet processing. LG has announced plans to commercialize dry electrode technology by 2028, with pilot lines already under construction at its Ochang facility. This technology is expected to reduce electrode manufacturing costs by 17–30 % while enabling higher energy density through thicker electrodes.

### Pre‑Dispersion in LG’s Process

The uniform dispersion of CNTs is a key quality gate in LG’s mixing process. The pre‑dispersion step—where CNTs, solvent, and dispersant are milled before adding active material—ensures that the final slurry has no agglomerates. This directly influences coating uniformity, electrode adhesion, and long‑term cycle stability. LG Energy Solution’s internal standards require that CNT dispersion quality be verified via particle size analysis and rheology measurements before moving to the coating stage.
