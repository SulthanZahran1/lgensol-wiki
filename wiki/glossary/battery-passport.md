# Battery Passport

## Overview

A battery passport is a digital record that captures the complete lifecycle data of a single battery — from raw material extraction through manufacturing, first use, second-life applications, and eventual recycling. The concept was first proposed in 2020 by the Global Battery Alliance (GBA), a public–private collaboration platform incubated at the World Economic Forum. The GBA’s goal was to promote resource circularity and supply chain transparency across the rapidly growing battery industry.

The European Union (EU) incorporated the battery passport into its new Battery Regulation (EU 2023/1542), making it mandatory from **February 2027** for all batteries placed on the EU market with a storage capacity exceeding 2 kWh. This covers electric vehicle (EV) batteries, industrial batteries, and light means of transport (LMT) batteries. The passport is accessed via a scannable QR code affixed to the battery, linking to a digital platform where authorized stakeholders can view relevant, role-based information.

## Technical Details

A battery passport functions as a digital twin of the physical battery. It is built on a decentralized or federated data architecture — often using blockchain technology — to ensure immutability, auditability, and resistance to tampering. LG Energy Solution, for example, has operated a blockchain-based platform since 2020 for tracking cobalt supply chain data, automatically linking raw material inbound and production outbound records.

The passport contains several categories of data, as defined by the GBA and EU regulation:

- **Manufacturer Information**: battery classification (e.g., EV, industrial), unique identifier, manufacturing date, and production facility location.
- **Technical Specifications**: weight, capacity (kWh), cell chemistry type (e.g., NMC, LFP), voltage, and appropriate fire extinguishing agents for safety.
- **Supply Chain Data**: origin of raw materials such as lithium, cobalt, nickel, and graphite; supply chain due diligence reports; and hazardous substance content.
- **Sustainability Metrics**: product carbon footprint (calculated via life-cycle assessment), percentage of recycled content, and ESG performance scores based on standards like GRI, SASB, and TCFD.
- **Lifecycle History**: usage patterns, number of charge–discharge cycles, refurbishment events, second-life application status (e.g., stationary energy storage), and final recycling outcomes (material recovery rates).

The passport enables role-based access: regulators verify compliance; recyclers obtain safe handling and disassembly instructions; second-life operators assess remaining capacity and state-of-health; and consumers see environmental and social impact data to make informed purchasing decisions.

## Significance and LG Energy Solution Context

Battery passports are a cornerstone of the transition to a circular battery economy. They support informed decision-making at every lifecycle stage, drive design-for-recycling, and enable accurate carbon footprint accounting. For manufacturers, passports provide a structured framework for demonstrating compliance with evolving regulations — especially the EU’s requirements on recycled content and due diligence.

**LG Energy Solution’s Leadership**
LG Energy Solution joined the GBA in 2022, becoming the first global battery company to participate in a battery passport pilot project. The company achieved a key milestone in 2021–2022 by becoming the first Korean battery maker to engage in the GBA’s battery passport pilot. In 2025, at InterBattery Europe, LG unveiled a pilot version of its **Battery Regulation Management (BRM)** system. The BRM platform is designed to integrate battery passport data with the company’s broader digital ecosystem, including the [[baas|B.around Total Solution (BMTS)]] and [[baas|Battery as a Service (BaaS)]] offerings.

LG Energy Solution’s passport-related work aligns with its ESG vision, “We CHARGE toward a better future,” which includes 8 focus areas such as **Climate Action & Circular Economy** and **Responsible & Impactful Business**. Specifically, the company aims to build a **Perfect Closed Loop** by 2025 — covering raw material sourcing, production, consumption, and disposal/recycling. The battery passport is a key enabler of this closed-loop system, providing the data to trace recycled content and monitor supply chain ESG risks.

By 2030, LG Energy Solution targets 90% of its supplier group to be classified as low ESG risk, and the passport’s transparency supports this through third-party verification of responsible sourcing. The company also applies the passport framework to its end-of-life battery management, linking to [[battery-recycling|reuse and recycling]] initiatives.
