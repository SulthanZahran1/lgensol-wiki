# Quaternary Battery

## Overview

A **quaternary battery** refers to a lithium‑ion cell that employs a cathode material composed of **four metallic elements**—typically Nickel (Ni), Cobalt (Co), Manganese (Mn), and Aluminum (Al)—abbreviated as **NCMA**. This chemistry builds upon the well‑established ternary NCM (Ni‑Co‑Mn) and NCA (Ni‑Co‑Al) systems by adding a fourth element to simultaneously improve energy density, structural stability, safety, and cost. The NCMA cathode is a key enabler of today’s high‑performance electric vehicles (EVs) and represents the state of the art in commercial high‑nickel cathode technology.

While earlier cathodes used three active metals, the introduction of a small amount of aluminum—typically around 1 mol%—provides a unique balance that pushes nickel content toward 89% or higher without sacrificing cycle life or thermal stability. This makes the quaternary system a cornerstone of LG Energy Solution’s **Premium** battery segment, targeting the longest driving range, fastest charging, and highest power output.

## Technical Details

### Composition and Structure

The NCMA cathode is a layered oxide with the general formula Li[NiₓCoᵧMn_εAl_δ]O₂, where the most common commercial formulation is **NCMA89**: approximately 89% Ni, 5% Co, 5% Mn, and 1% Al. This composition is derived from high‑nickel NCM811 (80% Ni) by further increasing nickel while reducing cobalt and adding a small Al dopant.

The crystal structure remains the same α‑NaFeO₂ layered type as NCM and NCA, which is essential for reversible lithium intercalation. The addition of aluminum serves multiple purposes:

- **Structural stabilization**: Al³⁺ ions occupy transition‑metal sites in the lattice, strengthening the metal–oxygen bonds. This suppresses the unwanted H2–H3 phase transition—a volume change that occurs during deep delithiation and causes microcracking in high‑nickel cathodes.
- **Thermal stability**: The stronger Al–O bond resists oxygen release at elevated temperatures, reducing the risk of thermal runaway.
- **Cobalt reduction**: By substituting expensive and supply‑constrained cobalt with inexpensive aluminum, the material cost is lowered while maintaining structural integrity.

### Electrochemical Performance

NCMA cathodes achieve reversible capacities exceeding 220 mAh/g at 4.3 V (vs. Li/Li⁺), enabling cell energy densities above 800 Wh/L at the pouch‑cell level. The high nickel content provides high specific capacity, while cobalt and manganese help to maintain structural reversibility and safety. The aluminum dopant further reduces the anisotropic lattice strain that causes particle cracking in earlier high‑nickel systems.

### Challenges and Mitigation Strategies

Despite its advantages, the NCMA cathode faces several well‑known degradation mechanisms:

1. **Cation mixing** – Ni²⁺ and Li⁺ have similar ionic radii; at very high nickel content, nickel can occupy lithium sites, blocking diffusion pathways. This reduces rate capability and capacity.
2. **Surface reactivity** – Ni⁴⁺ at the charged state is highly oxidizing, catalyzing electrolyte decomposition and forming a thick, resistive [[cei|CEI (Cathode Electrolyte Interphase)]] layer.
3. **Microcracking** – Repeated volume changes during cycling cause grain boundary separation, exposing fresh surface to electrolyte and accelerating degradation.

To overcome these, multiple advanced engineering techniques are applied:

- **Bulk doping**: Small amounts of dopants (e.g., Zr, Al, B) are incorporated into the lattice to strengthen metal–oxygen bonds and suppress phase transitions and cation mixing.
- **Surface coating**: Ultrathin coatings (e.g., Al₂O₃, TiO₂, or lithium borate) are applied to the cathode particles. These coatings act as a physical barrier between the electrode and electrolyte, reducing oxygen release and side reactions, and stabilizing the CEI.
- **Single‑crystal morphology**: Instead of polycrystalline secondary particles (agglomerates of smaller primary crystals), single‑crystal NCMA particles are grown. These monolithic particles eliminate internal grain boundaries, drastically reducing microcracking and improving long‑term cycle life. LG Energy Solution has advanced from small‑particle single crystals to medium‑sized single crystals, increasing electrode packing density and cell energy density.

The formation and stability of the CEI are especially crucial for quaternary cathodes operating at high voltages (>4.35 V). A well‑constructed, inorganic‑rich CEI (containing LiF, Li₂CO₃, Li₂O) minimizes dissolution of transition metals (Ni, Co, Mn) into the electrolyte and prevents their deposition on the anode, thereby preserving capacity and safety.

## Significance and LG Energy Solution Context

Quaternary batteries occupy the **Premium** tier of LG Energy Solution’s three‑segment EV strategy (Premium, Standard, Affordable). For customers demanding the longest driving range (over 600 km per charge) and fastest charging (<18 min to 80%), NCMA is the cathode of choice. It outperforms mid‑nickel NCM (e.g., NCM523) and NCA in energy density while offering better safety than very high‑nickel (≥95% Ni) alternatives.

LG Energy Solution has been a pioneer in commercializing NCMA, integrating it into both pouch and cylindrical form factors (e.g., 21700 cells). The company’s proprietary single‑crystal technology, combined with advanced coating and doping, has enabled NCMA cells to meet stringent automotive durability targets (e.g., 1,000 cycles with >80% retention). This expertise is built on decades of cathode material synthesis and battery manufacturing, and it positions LG as a leader in the high‑energy‑density segment.

The quaternary system also contributes to supply‑chain resilience. By reducing cobalt content from the 15–20% level in older NCM111 to below 5% in NCMA89, dependence on critical minerals is lowered. Aluminum is abundant and ethically sourced, improving the sustainability profile of the battery.
