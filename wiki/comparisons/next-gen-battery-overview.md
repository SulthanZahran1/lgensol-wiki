# Next-Generation Battery Comprehensive Comparison

## Overview / Introduction

The lithium-ion battery (LIB) has dominated the energy storage landscape since its commercialization in 1991, but its performance and cost limits, along with resource constraints, are driving the development of multiple next-generation chemistries. No single technology is expected to replace LIB across all applications; instead, a portfolio of systems tailored to specific needs—safety, energy density, weight, cost, or supply-chain resilience—is emerging. This page compares the principal routes under investigation by LG Energy Solution and the broader industry: solid-state, lithium-sulfur, lithium-metal, sodium-ion, lithium-manganese-rich (LMR), and bipolar architectures. Each technology addresses a distinct bottleneck, and their commercial timelines span 2026 to 2030+.

## Technical Details

### Solid-State Battery (SSB)

Solid-state batteries replace the liquid electrolyte with a solid ion conductor—typically sulfide, oxide, or polymer. Sulfide electrolytes (e.g., Li<sub>6</sub>PS<sub>5</sub>Cl) offer high ionic conductivity ( >1 mS/cm) but are moisture-sensitive; oxide electrolytes (e.g., LLZO) provide better stability but higher interfacial resistance. The solid electrolyte also acts as a physical separator, eliminating the need for a polymer membrane and allowing a lithium metal anode, which can theoretically deliver a 2–3× increase in volumetric energy density. Key challenges include suppressing lithium dendrite growth along grain boundaries, managing stack pressure (usually 1–10 MPa), and scaling manufacturing of thin, defect-free electrolyte layers. LG Energy Solution is developing both sulfide and oxide routes in collaboration with the University of California San Diego and aims for initial commercialization between 2027 and 2030.

### Lithium-Sulfur Battery (Li–S)

Lithium-sulfur cells pair a sulfur composite cathode with a lithium metal anode. Sulfur’s low equivalent weight (32 g/mol) and multi-electron reduction (S<sub>8</sub> → Li<sub>2</sub>S) yield a theoretical gravimetric energy density of ~2600 Wh/kg; practical cells are expected to reach 400–600 Wh/kg, roughly 1.5× that of state-of-the-art LIBs. The cathode operates through a “stepwise conversion” mechanism involving soluble lithium polysulfide intermediates (Li<sub>2</sub>S<sub>x</sub>, x = 4–8). Polysulfide shuttling—dissolution and migration to the anode—causes capacity fade and low Coulombic efficiency. LG Energy Solution mitigates this with tailored electrolyte formulations and encapsulated carbon–sulfur composites. The company has demonstrated pouch-type Li–S cells for aviation and urban air mobility, leveraging the system’s light weight and high specific energy. Timeline: 2028+ for niche (e.g., drone) applications.

### Lithium Metal Battery (LMB)

Lithium metal anodes replace graphite, increasing anode theoretical capacity from 372 mAh/g to 3,860 mAh/g. When paired with conventional liquid electrolytes, LMBs can achieve energy densities above 350 Wh/kg at the cell level. The primary challenge is dendrite formation during plating/stripping: uneven deposition leads to needle-like structures that can pierce the separator and cause internal short circuits. Additionally, the high reactivity of lithium consumes electrolyte, forming a fragile solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) that degrades Coulombic efficiency. LG Energy Solution, through a Frontier Research Laboratory with KAIST (established 2021), developed a borate–pyran-based liquid electrolyte that reconstructs the SEI into a dense, corrosion-resistant layer. This technology demonstrated 900 km of driving range on a single charge (50% improvement over high-performance LIBs) with >400 cycles. LMBs can leverage existing LIB manufacturing infrastructure, accelerating their path to market. Target initial deployment: 2026–2028.

### Sodium-Ion Battery (Na-ion)

Sodium-ion cells operate on the same rocking‑chair principle as LIBs but use Na⁺ ions instead of Li⁺. Cathode materials include layered sodium transition-metal oxides (Na<sub>x</sub>TMO<sub>2</sub>), polyanion compounds, and Prussian blue analogs. The anode is typically hard carbon (disordered sp<sup>2</sup> carbon with larger interlayer spacing than graphite) because Na⁺ ions (radius ~1.02 Å) do not intercalate efficiently into graphite. Hard carbon delivers 300–350 mAh/g but suffers from low initial Coulombic efficiency (80–90%, vs. >95% for graphite LIBs). Na-ion cells achieve 140–160 Wh/kg, lower than mainstream LIBs, but excel in low‑temperature performance: at –20 °C, they retain ~90% of room‑temperature capacity versus 60–70% for LIBs. The use of aluminum current collectors on both anode and cathode (copper is not needed) reduces cost. LG Energy Solution disclosed its 1<sup>st</sup>‑generation Na‑ion roadmap at InterBattery 2025, targeting stationary storage and lead‑acid replacement by 2027, followed by low‑cost EV applications from 2028.

### Lithium Manganese Rich (LMR) Battery

LMR cathodes (xLi<sub>2</sub>MnO<sub>3</sub>·(1−x)LiMO<sub>2</sub>, with M = Ni, Co, Mn) contain 60–65% manganese, eliminating cobalt and significantly reducing material cost. They deliver energy densities comparable to NCM (≥250 Wh/kg) at a cost approaching LFP. The primary degradation mechanism is voltage fade—a continuous drop in operating voltage during cycling caused by structural transformation into a spinel‑like phase. Low initial Coulombic efficiency (first‑cycle loss) also limits practical capacity. LG Energy Solution develops LMR in prismatic formats for GM, targeting commercial introduction after 2027.

### Bipolar Technology

Bipolar stacking connects cells in series through shared bipolar plates, reducing current collector, tab, and housing volume by up to 80%. This architecture simplifies high‑voltage pack construction and improves thermal management by reducing heat generation hotspots. LG Energy Solution explores bipolar designs for semi‑solid and solid‑state batteries, aiming for automotive packs with reduced inactive mass. Timeline: 2028+.

## Significance / LG Context

LG Energy Solution’s diverse next‑generation portfolio reflects a strategy of parallel development rather than betting on a single successor to LIB. The company leverages its core competencies in pouch‑cell manufacturing (Li‑S), prismatic large‑format cells (LMR), and electrolyte engineering (LMB). External partnerships with UCSD (solid‑state), KAIST (lithium metal), and GM (LMR) de‑risk fundamental challenges. Na‑ion and LMR address cost and supply‑chain security, while solid‑state and Li‑S target the highest safety and specific energy, respectively. The bipolar platform provides a horizontal enabling technology for all chemistries. Commercial rollouts are staggered: LMB and Na‑ion stationary first (2026–2027), followed by SSB, Li‑S, and LMR in later years.
