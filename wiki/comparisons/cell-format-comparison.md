# Cell Form Factor Comparison (Cylindrical vs Pouch vs Prismatic)

## Overview

Lithium-ion cells are manufactured in three primary physical formats—cylindrical, pouch, and prismatic—each distinguished by its mechanical structure, manufacturing process, and system-level tradeoffs. The choice of form factor directly affects volumetric energy density, thermal behavior, mechanical robustness, pack integration complexity, and cost. LG Energy Solution (LGES) is the only global battery manufacturer with an active portfolio spanning all three form factors, positioning the company to meet diverse customer requirements across electric vehicles (EVs), energy storage systems (ESS), and consumer electronics.

## Technical Details

### Cylindrical Cells

Cylindrical cells are the oldest and most standardized format. They are produced by winding electrode sheets and separators into a jelly-roll using a winder, inserting the roll into a rigid metal can (typically steel or aluminum), and then adding tabs (aluminum for the cathode, copper for the anode) followed by electrolyte filling and sealing. Key sizes include the widely used **18650** (18 mm diameter × 65 mm height) and **21700** (21 mm × 70 mm), as well as the emerging **46-series** (46 mm diameter; heights from 80 mm to 120 mm, e.g., 4680 and 4695). The rigid can provides excellent mechanical strength and internal pressure resistance. A built-in Current Interrupt Device (CID) and vent mechanism safely disconnect the circuit and release gas under overpressure conditions, enhancing safety. The circular cross-section, however, leaves unused space in packs, limiting volumetric efficiency. Cylindrical cells offer very high manufacturing speed due to mature, standardized equipment—an advantage that becomes more pronounced in large-format 46-series cells, where fewer cells per pack are needed (e.g., ~828 4680 cells replace ~4,400 21700 cells in a typical EV pack).

### Pouch Cells

Pouch cells use a flexible, aluminum-laminated film pouch as the casing. Electrodes are stacked rather than wound: LGES’s proprietary **Advanced Z-Stacking (AZS)** technology laminates and stacks unit cells (mono-cells) comprising separator–electrode–separator layers, then assembles them into a final stack. This construction eliminates the empty space inherent in cylindrical cans, yielding the highest **volumetric energy density** among the three formats. Pouch cells can be made very thin and in custom shapes to fit specific device cavities—crucial for slim laptops and smartphones. However, they require external mechanical support (e.g., rigid pack frames) to prevent swelling and are more vulnerable to puncture. LGES is the world’s leading pouch cell manufacturer, supplying Ultium Cells (with GM), NextStar Energy (with Stellantis), and its Honda joint venture. The AZS process ensures precise electrode alignment, which is critical for high-capacity, high-safety automotive cells.

### Prismatic Cells

Prismatic cells are enclosed in a rectangular metal (usually aluminum) or plastic case. The internal electrode assembly can be wound (jelly-roll) or stacked. LGES is developing prismatic cells using its advanced stacking technology, which overcomes the historical drawback of empty corner space in wound prismatics. The rigid rectangular case provides high mechanical strength, good swelling control, and easy module assembly. Prismatic cells offer a good balance between volumetric efficiency and structural integrity. LGES has signed a joint development agreement with GM for prismatic cells intended for GM’s next-generation EVs, marking the company’s official entry into this format and completion of its “all three form factors” strategy.

## Significance / LG Context

LGES’s unique position as the only battery manufacturer with a commercial portfolio covering **cylindrical, pouch, and prismatic** cells is a strategic differentiator. Each format serves distinct market segments:

- **Cylindrical cells** are favored for high-volume, standardized EV platforms (e.g., Rivian’s 4695 cells) and power tools, offering fast production rates and inherent safety.
- **Pouch cells** dominate in applications demanding the highest energy density and design flexibility—from EVs (GM Ultium, Stellantis) to portable electronics.
- **Prismatic cells** are increasingly adopted by automotive OEMs seeking structural simplicity and robust thermal management, especially in cell-to-pack (CTP) architectures.

LGES’s investment in **46-series cylindrical production** (e.g., Arizona facility) and **prismatic R&D with GM** demonstrates the company’s commitment to covering the full spectrum of customer needs. Additionally, LGES’s high-precision **AZS stacking** technology—originally developed for pouch cells—is now being leveraged for prismatic cells, ensuring industry-leading electrode alignment and yield.
